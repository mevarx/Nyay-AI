Core Concepts

Six things you must understand before deploying any AI decision system

What is Algorithmic Bias?

Algorithmic bias occurs when a model reflects unacknowledged prejudices that systematically disadvantage certain groups. It's rarely intentional — it's a byproduct of learning from historical data that already contains inequality.

Key Takeaway: AI does not remove human bias. By default, it automates and scales it.

The Proxy Variable Problem

Even if you delete "Caste" or "Gender" from your dataset, an AI can infer them through proxies — last name, pin code, school name, or mother tongue. Removing the label doesn't remove the signal.

Key Takeaway: Deleting sensitive columns isn't enough. You must audit for what the remaining columns are correlated with.

Why India Needs Its Own Tools

Most fairness tools are built in the West, where bias is analyzed through race, gender, and age. India's fabric is uniquely complex — caste, religion, region, and linguistic heritage all operate as invisible axes of discrimination that Western metrics completely miss.

Key Takeaway: You cannot fix Indian bias with Western metrics. It must be audited through India's specific social lens.

Types of Bias in Data

Bias enters through three main doors: historical bias (the world is already unfair), representation bias (dataset is unbalanced — e.g., only 5% rural data), and measurement bias (you're asking the wrong questions).

Key Takeaway: Bias usually stems from flawed data collection, not flawed math. Fix the data first.

Legal Risk in India

India's constitution guarantees equality under Articles 14–17. Algorithmic decision systems that produce discriminatory outcomes for protected groups may violate these provisions. The DPDP Act 2023 further regulates automated processing of personal data.

Key Takeaway: Biased AI isn't just unethical — it can be legally actionable under Indian law.
